On Choosing Wood:
A Beginner's Confusion

Shujauddin 15 March 2025 6 min read

I spent three weeks going to the same timber yard before I finally understood what the grain was telling me. Every time I walked in, the owner would glance up from his chai and ask, "Kya chahiye aaj?" — and every time I'd say something vague like "something for a small stand" and he'd hand me a piece of sheesham that, while beautiful, was completely wrong for what I had in mind.

The problem wasn't the wood. The problem was me. I didn't know how to look.

The Grain is Not Decoration

This is the first thing I got wrong. I chose wood the way I'd choose a tile — for the surface pattern. I liked the dark streaks in walnut, the pale smoothness of maple. I wasn't thinking about what those patterns meant structurally: where the fibres were running, how the piece would move when the seasons changed, where it was likely to split under stress.

"Grain isn't just what the wood looks like — it's a map of how it was once alive."
1 / 3

Left to right: face grain, end grain, and quarter-sawn surface on the same walnut plank.

Once I started seeing grain as structure rather than texture, everything changed. I began to understand why quarter-sawn oak looks different from flat-sawn oak — and why the woodworker at the yard always asked "kya banana hai?" before recommending anything. He needed to know the use case, not the aesthetic preference.

Hard vs Soft: It's More Complicated

The hardwood/softwood distinction sounds useful until you learn that balsa — the lightest, flimsiest wood you can buy — is technically a hardwood. The classification is botanical, not mechanical. Hardwoods come from flowering trees (angiosperms); softwoods come from conifers. That's it.

Teak (left) vs pine (right) — both beautiful, very different in density and workability.

What actually matters for workshop use is the Janka hardness rating — a measure of how much force it takes to embed a small steel ball into the wood's surface. Harder isn't always better. For earrings, I want something that can be cut fine without splintering. For a laptop stand bearing weight, I want density and stability.

The Woods I Now Keep in the Workshop

After three years of experimenting, I've settled into a small rotation. Sheesham for large surfaces — it's affordable, works well with hand tools, and the grain is striking. Walnut for jewelry — it's dense enough to hold fine detail, and the dark tone looks stunning against skin. Teak for anything that needs to handle moisture or temperature changes. Bamboo for quick functional pieces — it's not technically a wood, but it behaves like one and it's remarkably consistent.

Walnut earring blank · Sheesham plank · Bamboo section

The most important lesson I took from those early weeks of confusion: go to the timber yard with a sketch or a photo, not just a vague description. Let the material surprise you, but give it a problem to solve first. That's when wood stops being decoration and starts becoming craft.
