Tools

The Five Chisels
I Actually Use

Shujauddin2 Feb 20254 min read

I own around thirty chisels. I use five of them regularly. If you're starting out and wondering what to buy, here's the shortlist — with no brand affiliations and no hedging.

"Buy fewer, better. A sharp cheap chisel beats a dull expensive one, but a sharp good chisel changes how you think about wood."

The working set (left) and the sharpening station that keeps them that way (right).

The Five

01

6mm Bench Chisel

The detail worker. This gets into mortises, cleans up dovetail corners, and does all the fiddly stuff on jewelry pieces. I've rehandled mine twice — the blade is worth keeping.

02

12mm Bench Chisel

The workhorse. This is the chisel I reach for first on almost every project. Fits most mortise widths I work with, handles paring duty, and is easy to keep sharp.

03

25mm Bench Chisel

For wide paring cuts, cleaning up large tenon cheeks, and flattening. Also pulls double duty as a scraper when I'm too lazy to reach for one.

04

Skew Chisel (any width)

I resisted this one for two years and then couldn't believe I'd managed without it. The angled tip reaches into corners a bench chisel never can. Essential for cleaning up stopped dados.

05

Long Paring Chisel (any width)

Not for mallet work — this one's hand-pushed only. The extra length gives you control that a bench chisel can't match when you're levelling a long surface or sneaking up on a tight fit.

On Sharpening

None of this matters if you're not sharpening. I sharpen before every session, not after. My setup is a 1000-grit waterstone for repair work and a 6000-grit for polishing. A leather strop at the end. The whole thing takes under four minutes once it's a habit.

If you're only going to buy one thing after reading this: buy a good waterstone and sharpen whatever chisels you already have. You'll be surprised how much they improve.
